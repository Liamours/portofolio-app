CV Standards for AI Engineer, Data Scientist, Computer Vision, Medical Imaging Researcher
Sources: resumeworded.com, tealhq.com, beamjobs.com, towardsdatascience.com, opencv.org, turing.com, resumegenius.com, frontlinesourcegroup.com


SECTIONS

The field converges on seven sections. Order matters.

1. Contact header. Name, email, GitHub, LinkedIn. No photo, no address, no date of birth, no nationality. Some add a one-line title under the name.

2. Summary or objective. 2 to 4 lines maximum. States the role, the specialization, and one anchor achievement. Skipped entirely by many strong candidates; only keep it if it adds something the rest of the CV cannot communicate on its own.

3. Experience. The heaviest section. Ordered newest-first. Each entry: role title, organization, period in month-year format. 2 to 4 bullets per role. Internships are labeled as internships, full-time as full-time. Part-time and volunteer roles are included if the work is relevant.

4. Projects. Used when experience alone does not show enough technical depth. Each project gets one line of context and 1 to 3 bullets. Research projects with published outcomes belong here or in a separate publications section.

5. Publications. Standard in academic and research CVs. Format: authors, year, title, venue. Author order is stated as-is. IEEE, ACM, and Nature family venues carry the most weight. Preprints are included but labeled as preprints. Datasets released publicly are included as a separate entry.

6. Skills. A flat or lightly grouped list. Frameworks and tools listed explicitly. No soft skills. No "proficient in", "familiar with", "exposure to" — just list the tool or do not list it.

7. Education. Institution, degree, GPA if strong (3.5+), graduation year. No high school. Relevant coursework is rarely listed by experienced candidates.

Awards and competitions appear either as a subsection of experience or as a standalone section at the bottom. They are included only when the result is verifiable and named: placement, prize, or official qualification. "Participated in" is not included.


HOW BULLETS ARE WRITTEN

The dominant pattern is: verb, what was built or changed, measured result.

"Trained a segmentation model on 3,593 bone scan images. Achieved DSC 0.934 across 12 skeletal regions, surpassing two commercially deployed systems."

Bullets start with a past-tense action verb for completed work, present-tense for ongoing. One sentence per bullet is the norm. Two sentences if the second gives the result and cannot be merged cleanly.

Every bullet either names a metric or names a named system, dataset, or benchmark. "Improved accuracy" without a number is a red flag. "Improved accuracy by 44.1% on the endemic species subset" is correct.

The metric comes at the end of the bullet, not the beginning.

Tools and models named explicitly inside bullets: not "used a deep learning framework" but "fine-tuned BirdNET v2.4 with MixIT source separation." This matters for ATS and for technical reviewers both.


LEVEL OF DETAIL

AI Engineer and Data Scientist CVs go one level deeper than general software roles. Architecture names are acceptable: YOLOv8, nnU-Net v2, DenseNet121. Dataset names are acceptable: VinBig, NIH ChestX-ray14. Benchmark names are expected: mAP@0.5, AUC, F1.

Medical imaging and research CVs go one level further: clinical collaborators and hospitals are named when the work involved them. Funding bodies are named. Journal and conference names are written in full on first reference.

The ceiling is: any detail that cannot be verified by a reviewer, or that only makes sense to the person who wrote the code, is cut. Internal variable names, specific hyperparameter values, exact Python version, and lines of code are not included.


WHAT IS DISPLAYED

Quantified outcomes. The single most consistent finding across all sources is that every strong bullet has at least one number.

Named tools, models, and benchmarks. Specificity signals depth.

Institutional and organizational names. Dr. Hasan Sadikin Hospital, Universitas Padjadjaran, IEEE Xplore — these names function as implicit verification.

Funding and collaboration context. "Ministry of Higher Education-funded" is one phrase that does real work.

Competition results with placement. 3rd place, Best Paper, national qualifier — all fine if verifiable.

GitHub links. Standard for open-source or public research work.

Publications list. Even a preprint or dataset counts and should be listed.


WHAT IS NOT DISPLAYED

Soft skills. "Strong communicator", "team player", "passionate about AI" — universally cited as red flags. None of the high-quality examples include them.

Skill lists that overflow one section. If the skills section takes more than 25% of the page it is a red flag. List only what you can be questioned on in an interview.

Duties without results. "Responsible for training models" with no metric attached reads as filler.

Tool versions in isolation. "Python 3.11", "Docker 24.0" — version numbers without context add nothing. Name the tool, skip the version.

Hardware specs. GPU model and VRAM are not CV content.

Lines of code as a metric. "Wrote 3,000 lines of code" appears in none of the strong examples. It measures effort, not outcome.

Grant IDs and internal tracking numbers. The funding body name is sufficient.

Team member names in bullet points. Each bullet describes the candidate's contribution, not the team roster.

High school. Never included.

Photo, nationality, date of birth, home address. Not included in tech CVs targeted at international or research audiences.

Objective statements that are generic. "Seeking a challenging role where I can apply my skills" is consistently listed as a red flag.

References section. "References available upon request" wastes space. Drop it.


RED FLAGS SPECIFIC TO THIS FIELD

Claiming a tool without being able to use it in an interview. If it is on the CV, it will be tested.

Describing a project without saying what your role was. "The team built X" — who are you in that sentence?

Listing model accuracy on training data. Validation or test set metrics only.

Accuracy on imbalanced datasets without mentioning the imbalance. Report F1 or AUC, not raw accuracy, when classes are unequal.

Publications listed without author order context when you are not the first author. "Second author" or "et al." is fine; omitting it when you did not lead the work is not.

Year gaps in experience without explanation. Reviewers notice. Either account for the period or leave it out and be ready to explain verbally.


FORMAT

One page for under 7 years of experience. Two pages for 7 or more. Never three.

PDF only. No Word files.

No graphics, no icons, no color except black. Clean sans-serif or serif font, consistent size, consistent alignment.

Margins 0.5 to 1 inch. No headers or footers with page numbers unless two pages.

Dates right-aligned against the organization name. Period formatted as "Mar 2025 – Present", not "03/25 – current."

ACADEMIC CV vs INDUSTRY RESUME

These are two different documents with different purposes. The confusion between them is the single most common mistake researchers make.

Academic CV: length is not penalized. 3 to 5 pages for a PhD student, 5 to 8 for a postdoc applying for faculty. Sections not found in industry: research interests statement, teaching philosophy, conference presentations, poster sessions, invited talks, service and committee work, mentorship, grants as PI or Co-I with dollar amounts, and references (2 to 4 named, with contact). Publications are expanded: each entry includes all authors, year, full title, journal, volume, page numbers, and DOI. Bold your own name in every author list.

Industry resume: 1 page under 7 years of experience, 2 pages for 7 or more. Publications appear but are compressed to 2 to 4 most relevant entries. Presentations and posters are dropped unless they were invited keynotes. Teaching appears only if it is directly relevant to the role. No references section. No research interests statement. Grants appear only as a credibility signal, not as a full funding history.

The key difference in framing: academic CV asks "what have you contributed to knowledge?" Industry resume asks "what can you change in our system?" The same experience needs to be written differently for each. A bone scan segmentation project on an academic CV leads with the methodology and the novelty of the approach. On an industry resume it leads with the production outcome: a pipeline that outperformed two commercial systems.

For someone at the research-to-industry boundary, the standard recommendation is to maintain both documents and know which one to send.


BULLET POINT PATTERNS: WEAK VS STRONG

The pattern is consistent across all sources. The failure mode is describing what the role was, not what was produced.

Weak: "Built a segmentation model for bone scans."
Strong: "Trained nnU-Net v2 for skeletal segmentation across 12 anatomical regions. IoU 0.879, surpassing EXINI and BONENAVI at patient-level AUC."

Weak: "Worked on bird sound classification."
Strong: "Fine-tuned BirdNET v2.4 on 12,511 recordings across 219 endemic and endangered species. Macro F1 improved 44.1% over baseline. Validated in field conditions at two zoos."

Weak: "Deployed a fatigue detection API."
Strong: "Deployed a 3-service async API against live company MDVR server. Macro F1 0.78, 11% above the model in production."

Weak: "Reviewed literature for the research project."
Strong: "Screened 212 papers using Zotero and Parsifal. Applied quality assessment and data extraction to produce a structured trend analysis by publication year."

The structural rule: one metric or one named verifiable outcome per bullet. If neither exists, the bullet should not exist.

One additional pattern that appears in strong ML/AI resumes: when there is a comparison point, use it. "Surpasses X and Y" is stronger than "achieves Z." "11% gain over the production model" is stronger than "Macro F1 0.78." The comparison gives the reader a reference frame without requiring domain knowledge.


STUDENT AND EARLY-CAREER SPECIFIC PATTERNS

Without industry experience, the ordering changes. For students and researchers:

Projects move to the second section, immediately after education, ahead of any part-time or committee roles. The projects section carries the weight that experience carries for senior candidates.

Each project entry follows the same bullet structure as an experience entry. Role stated explicitly: "Lead Coder", "Research Lead", "Module Lead". Task and outcome follow. The common mistake is listing a project without stating what your specific contribution was.

Competitions with named placements are included under a separate awards section, not buried in experience. "3rd place, CCI Summit Kaggle Data Research Competition, 2024" is one line. The detail of what was done during the competition goes in the projects section if the project is substantial enough to list.

Internships are labeled as internships, not as "experience." The period is given in month-year format. For short internships (under 3 months), one or two bullets maximum.

Funding and institutional affiliation signal that the work was real and reviewed. "Ministry of Higher Education RI-funded" in a project entry signals external validation. "Supervised by nuclear medicine physicians at Dr. Hasan Sadikin Hospital" signals clinical grounding. These phrases should appear when true.

Publications are listed even as a student. A single IEEE paper as first author at the undergraduate level is notable and should be prominent, not buried.


PUBLICATIONS SECTION FORMAT

The standard for technical and research CVs is consistent. Two common formats:

For academic CVs, full citation: Authors (bold your name). Year. Title. Journal/Conference, Volume(Issue), Pages. DOI or URL.

For industry resumes, compressed: Title. Venue (abbreviated). Year. Link.

Points on author order: first author is the primary researcher. Corresponding author signals responsibility for the work. Middle authors contributed but did not lead. Last author is often the supervisor or PI. When listing publications where you are not first author, the standard is to still list in full but it is understood that the contribution weight is different.

Preprints are listed, labeled as preprints, with the preprint server named: SSRN, arXiv, bioRxiv. "Under review" is acceptable to state if it is true and the submission date is included.

Datasets released publicly count as research outputs and belong in the publications section under a separate type label.

Conference papers are included even when the conference is not the most prestigious. The IEEE Xplore indexing matters. A published, indexed conference paper is different from an abstract or a poster.


SKILLS SECTION DISCIPLINE

The skills section is where most candidates oversell and undersell simultaneously. Overselling: listing a tool or framework after one tutorial or a single project. Underselling: leaving off core tools used daily because they seem obvious.

The discipline comes from one test: could you be interviewed on this today? If yes, it belongs on the CV. If no, it does not.

Grouping is acceptable but not required. The most common groupings are: languages, frameworks, tools, platforms, domain-specific. No group should exceed 8 items.

"Familiar with" and "exposure to" are never written. Either you know it well enough to use it under interview conditions or you do not list it.

Research-specific tools that are often omitted but should be included when used seriously: Zotero, Parsifal, MATLAB, DICOM processing libraries, annotation tools, clinical data standards. These signal domain expertise that general ML candidates lack.

Skills rated by level (expert, intermediate, beginner) are a debated practice. Most strong CVs in this field do not rate skills. Grouping by priority (primary skills, secondary skills) is a softer and less risky alternative.


WHAT INTERVIEWERS ACTUALLY CHECK

From practitioner accounts: resumes are reviewed for approximately 6 to 7 seconds in the first pass. The eye goes to: most recent role title and organization, one or two numbers visible in the bullet points, the skills section, highest education.

If those pass, the second read is looking for: whether the bullet points are tasks or outcomes, whether the metrics are specific or vague, whether the claimed tools match the context (claiming nnU-Net with no segmentation project is a red flag), whether there is a consistent narrative of growth.

Questions that follow a strong CV bullet: "Walk me through the model architecture", "What was the baseline you were comparing against", "How did you handle class imbalance", "What would you do differently." These are not trick questions — they are verification questions. Every bullet should be defensible in 2 minutes of technical conversation.

The single most credible signal on a student or early-career CV in this field: a production system, a published paper, or a dataset released publicly. Any one of these converts the CV from a list of activities into evidence of real output.


WHAT IS ABSENT FROM STRONG CVS IN THIS FIELD

No objective statement. No "I am passionate about artificial intelligence and seek to make meaningful contributions." This language appears in none of the strong examples and is consistently cited as a red flag.

No references section with the phrase "available upon request." Drop it entirely.

No photo, age, gender, nationality, religion, marital status, or home address. These are not included in CVs targeting tech or research roles in any international context.

No high school information at any point after the first year of university.

No "Responsible for" as a bullet opener. Replace with an action verb and a result.

No duties listed without outcomes. "Assisted in data collection" is not a CV bullet. "Annotated 600+ bone scan images under clinical supervision" is.

No courses listed in the education section unless the course name directly names a skill not otherwise evidenced on the CV.

No soft skills listed by name: "strong communicator", "collaborative", "detail-oriented" appear in none of the strong examples. These qualities are demonstrated through the experience content, not stated explicitly.

No salary information, ever.

No references to why you left a previous role.
